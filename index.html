Steel varies by grade, temper, and bar size — treat these as practical workshop guidelines, not precise specs. Always do a test bend on scrap first.

Springback — always overbend

All steel springs back after bending due to elastic recovery. For mild steel expect 2–5° springback; for harder grades or high-tensile rebar this can reach 8–15°. The tighter the radius relative to bar diameter, the more springback you'll see. Bend, release, and measure — adjust your machine setting and repeat until you hit the target. Note this offset for future bends with the same bar and radius.

Bend Allowance — the bar lengthens

When a bar bends, the neutral axis (which neither stretches nor compresses) sits roughly 45–50% of the bar diameter from the inner face. The outer surface stretches and the inner compresses. For most practical CER40 work the effect is small — calculate your arc length at the centrelineradius and trim to final fit if precision is critical. For tight bends on thick bar, add a small allowance (roughly 0.5× bar diameter per 90°).

Minimum Bend Radius

Bending too tight will crack the outer surface. As a practical guide:
— Up to 12mm bar: minimum radius ≈ 2× bar diameter
— 16–25mm bar: minimum radius ≈ 3–4× bar diameter
— 25mm+ bar: minimum radius ≈ 4–6× bar diameter
The CER40's die set also sets a hard limit — your die bore diameter determines the tightest radius you can achieve for each bar size.

Measuring an Arch from a Template

If you have a physical arch to match (window head, door frame, decorative element), measure two things: the chord (straight line across the opening) and the rise (height from chord midpoint to arch peak). Enter both into the Arc Calculator — it will give you the exact radius and arc length to program into your bender. This is usually more accurate than trying to measure the radius directly.

Full Rings & Circles

For a complete ring, use the Circle Calculator — the circumference is your cut bar length. Add 1–2× bar diameter extra for weld overlap. You'll almost certainly need to overbend past 360° to account for springback, then clamp and allow the ring to relax to round. For large-diameter rings, make multiple progressive passes rather than trying to complete the bend in one go.

Multi-Segment Shapes

Use the Bar Length tab to plan shapes that mix straight sections with bends (arched gates, balustrade components, frames). Add each segment in order — the calculator sums the arc lengths for each curved section. Always mark your bar with a permanent marker at each bend start point before loading into the machine, and keep consistent feed direction to avoid twist.

CER40 Practical Tips

— Mark from the centre of the intended arc, not the end.
— Make progressive passes on long arcs — multiple lighter passes give a smoother, more consistent curve than one aggressive bend.
— Check your die/pin clearance before starting — the pin diameter affects the actual inner radius.
— Deburr and clean bar ends before loading to avoid scoring the die.
— For stainless or hardened steel, expect significantly more springback and consider annealing before bending.
