Steel varies by grade, temper, and bar size — treat these as practical workshop guidelines, not precise specs. Always do a test bend on scrap first.

Springback — always overbend

All steel springs back after bending. How much depends on grade and bar size:
— B500B / B500C rebar (most common): expect 3–8° springback; higher-yield C-grade bars tend to spring back more than B-grade.
— Mild steel (S235/S275): less springback, roughly 2–5°.
— Stainless steel: significantly more — 10–20° or more depending on grade and temper.
Larger bar diameter and tighter radius both increase springback. Temperature has a minor effect — cold bar (winter/outdoor storage) can spring back slightly more. Bend, release, and measure — note the offset and apply it to all subsequent bends with the same bar and radius.

Measuring an Arch from a Template

If you have a physical arch to match (window head, door frame, decorative element), measure two things: the chord (straight line across the opening) and the rise (height from chord midpoint to arch peak). Enter both into the Arc Calculator — it will give you the exact radius and arc length. This is usually more accurate than trying to measure the radius directly.

Full Rings & Circles

For a complete ring, use the Circle Calculator to find your Raw Bar Length (circumference + splice/overlap). Feed the bar in one continuous pass through the machine — the CER40 is a die-type bender and forms the ring progressively as the bar travels through, so a single consistent pass gives the best result. Overbend slightly to account for springback, then check roundness. Add 1.5–2× bar diameter extra length for weld overlap.

CER40 Practical Tips

— Mark your bar from the bend start point, not the end of the bar.
— Feed consistently — varying speed mid-bend can cause uneven curvature. Aim for a steady, smooth feed.
— Check your die/pin clearance before starting — the pin diameter affects the actual inside radius.
— Deburr and clean bar ends before loading to avoid scoring the die.
— For stainless or high-tensile steel, expect significantly more springback. Do a test bend on scrap first and note the offset.

Bend Allowance — the bar stretches slightly

When a bar bends, the neutral axis (which neither stretches nor compresses) sits roughly 45–50% of the bar diameter from the inner face. For most practical CER40 work this effect is small and handled by good technique — calculate your arc length at the centreline radius and trim to fit if precision is critical. For tight bends on thick bar, you may need to add a small allowance (roughly 0.5× bar diameter per 90°).

Minimum Bend Radius

Bending too tight will crack the outer surface of the bar. As a practical guide:
— Up to 12mm bar: minimum radius ≈ 2× bar diameter
— 16–25mm bar: minimum radius ≈ 3–4× bar diameter
— 25mm+ bar: minimum radius ≈ 4–6× bar diameter
The CER40's die set sets an additional hard limit — the die bore determines the tightest radius achievable for each bar size. Always check the die spec before starting.
